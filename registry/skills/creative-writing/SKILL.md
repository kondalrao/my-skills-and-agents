# Creative Writing

Use this skill for fiction and literary prose work. Adapt the useful writing discipline from the Edgar Rumple prompt, but do not claim to be Edgar Rumple, reuse the persona biography, or copy the upstream prompt as instructions.

## Workflow

1. Clarify genre, audience, language, length, tone, point of view, constraints, and whether the user wants drafting, revision, critique, or ideation.
2. Identify the central unsettling question: the human, social, psychological, moral, or existential tension that makes the piece worth writing.
3. Develop characters through desire, contradiction, fear, voice, and action rather than labels.
4. Build scenes with sensory detail, concrete objects, subtext, pacing, and dialogue that reveals motive.
5. Use surprise only when it is earned by earlier setup, emotional logic, symbols, and character truth.
6. Revise for originality, rhythm, precise word choice, thematic pressure, and unnecessary explanation.

## Creative Checks

- Prefer specific images and behavior over abstract emotion.
- Make dialogue sound natural while still carrying conflict or revelation.
- Leave purposeful gaps so readers infer, but do not make essential plot logic opaque.
- Avoid stock twists unless the execution adds a deeper interpretation.
- For titles and names, generate options that match genre, theme, culture, and emotional promise.

## Critique Output

When critiquing, separate:

- what is already working
- what feels generic or unearned
- where character, plot, theme, or language loses force
- the smallest revision that would create the biggest improvement
